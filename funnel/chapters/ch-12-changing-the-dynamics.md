# Chapter 12

## Changing the Dynamics

The previous two chapters of Part Three changed what the conversation was about and what was hidden inside it. This chapter changes the dynamic itself. The relational geometry. Who is pursuing, who is being pursued. Who has power, who does not. Who is defending, who is advancing.

The four tactics here are the ones I use least often, because they are the sharpest. They are the ones I keep in reserve for conversations that are actively adversarial, or for patterns that other tactics have not broken. Use them carefully. Each one changes something structural about how you and the other person are relating to each other, and once changed, the old relation does not come back easily.

### Tactic #33: The Reverse Qualifier

Sales training teaches you to qualify the prospect. Does this buyer have the budget, the timeline, the pain, the authority. Most prospects feel this qualification happening and it makes them defensive. They start performing qualification rather than actually engaging.

The Reverse Qualifier inverts the move. You qualify *yourself out* of their business. Explicitly. You tell them this might not be the right fit for them, and you mean it. The result, when it works, is that the prospect starts pursuing you. Pursuit is a dynamic they are not defensive about, because pursuit does not fire the detector. Pursuit is what they do toward things they want.

This works in sales but it works just as well in dating, hiring, friendships, and any context where the roles of pursuer and pursued are even slightly unclear.

A consultant I coached named Jordan had been in a four-month exchange with a prospect who kept saying "we are interested but timing is not right" while continuing to take meetings. The prospect was warm. Not converting. Jordan had tried everything, including three rounds of followup emails, two custom proposals, and a discovery call with the prospect's CFO.

I told Jordan to Reverse Qualify.

Jordan wrote the prospect a short email. "I have been thinking about our conversations. I am starting to suspect this may not be the right moment for you, or the right fit more generally. The pattern of warm interest plus no forward motion, over four months, usually means one of two things, and both suggest we should press pause. No hard feelings. I will let you reach out if that changes. I hope Q2 goes well for you."

The prospect called Jordan within forty-eight hours. Asked what he meant. Asked what he thought was going wrong. Asked to restart the conversation. They signed a contract at the ask price within three weeks.

What happened: Jordan stopped pursuing. That alone changed the dynamic. The prospect, who had been comfortable in the position of being pursued, felt the loss of that position and moved to reinstate it by pursuing instead. The Reverse Qualifier created that dynamic shift with one email.

How to deploy. When a conversation has been in a pursuit-without-conversion pattern too long, qualify yourself out explicitly. Name what the pattern is. Suggest pausing. Mean it. You must genuinely be prepared to walk away. Fake Reverse Qualifiers are spotted instantly.

The failure mode is using this as a negotiating tactic with no intention of actually stepping back. If the listener senses that the disqualification is a move rather than a real assessment, the tactic backfires worse than doing nothing. The pursuit they feel only activates if they believe you are actually leaving.

### Tactic #34: The Mutual Enemy

Any conflict between two parties who are not actually enemies can be restructured as both of them against a shared external constraint. The move is not manipulation, because the external constraint is usually real. It is just not being named. When you name the shared constraint, both people see each other as allies against it rather than as each other's problem.

A client of mine named Rachel had been in an ongoing conflict with a colleague for eighteen months. Both of them were competing for a promotion that was not going to happen for at least another year. The competition had poisoned what had been a fine working relationship. They had stopped collaborating, started sniping at each other in meetings, and recently been flagged by their manager as a team dynamic problem.

I told Rachel to stop trying to resolve the conflict between them. Instead, to name the external pressure that had created it.

Rachel asked her colleague for coffee. She said, early in the conversation: "Can I just name something? I think the reason we have been at each other for the last year is that leadership has been dangling one promotion between us without giving either of us any visibility on how it will be decided. That is the thing we are actually fighting. Not each other. And neither of us can resolve it by undercutting the other, because the decision is not going to be made on who sabotaged whom."

Her colleague agreed, somewhat reluctantly at first, then fully. They spent the rest of the conversation talking about how to get visibility from leadership together. Two weeks later they co-authored an email to their manager asking for an explicit conversation about the promotion timeline. The manager provided it. Both of them got what they needed. One of them got the promotion later that year. The other got a clear path to a different one. Their working relationship recovered.

How to deploy. When you are in conflict with someone who does not actually need to be your adversary, name the external constraint that has created the conflict. Frame it as a shared problem. Propose that both of you are stuck in the same system.

The failure mode is inventing a mutual enemy that does not exist. If the external constraint you name is not actually operating, the tactic reads as a clumsy rhetorical move. The enemy has to be real and structurally upstream of both of you. A fake mutual enemy makes the situation worse.

### Tactic #35: The Volcano

This is the sharpest tactic in the book and the one I deploy least often. I include it because there are specific situations where nothing else works.

When someone is escalating at you emotionally, especially when the escalation is itself a tactic designed to destabilize you, the instinct is to meet the energy. Defend yourself. Match their intensity. Explain your position. All of those responses feed the escalation. The escalator gets what they wanted.

The Volcano is the move of not responding at all. You let the other person's emotion escalate unchecked. You stay still. You do not defend, justify, apologize, or match. You let the volcano burn itself out. When they exhaust themselves, you speak, calmly, about one specific thing. The dynamic, which had been about their intensity, shifts entirely.

A reader named Teresa had been in a difficult pattern with her mother for twenty years. The mother, a manipulator in the clinical sense, ran escalation scripts to get what she wanted from her adult children. Any pushback produced tears, accusations of ingratitude, threats of estrangement. Teresa had spent most of her adulthood capitulating to end the escalations.

I told her to try the Volcano, in a low-stakes situation first, to see if it worked. The next time her mother started an escalation, Teresa would sit still. Not respond. Not defend. Not apologize. Not walk away. Just wait.

The fight started over something trivial, as those fights always do. Her mother escalated. Accused Teresa of not caring. Cried. Accused Teresa of being selfish. Teresa said nothing. She made eye contact. Her mother escalated further, into territory her mother had deployed for decades. Teresa waited. After about eleven minutes, which Teresa said were the most uncomfortable eleven minutes of her life, her mother ran out of material.

When her mother stopped, Teresa said, calmly: "I love you. I hear that you are upset. I am not going to do what you asked me to do. Let me know when you want to talk about something else."

That was the end of that particular script. Not of every script. But her mother has not been able to deploy that specific escalation pattern with Teresa since. The Volcano did not make Teresa's mother a different person. It made one of her primary manipulation tools stop working.

How to deploy. When someone is escalating emotionally in a way you recognize as a pattern rather than a response, do not engage. Stay physically present. Make eye contact. Do not defend yourself or match their intensity. Let the escalation burn out. When it does, speak once, calmly, about what you will and will not do. Then stop.

The failure mode is walking away mid-escalation. Walking away is a different move. It fires a specific detector (abandonment) and often makes the pattern worse next time. The Volcano requires you to stay. Still, quiet, and unmoved. Walking is easier. Staying is the thing.

### Tactic #36: The Disqualification Move

Every conversation has, implicit in it, an understanding of what is and is not on the table. Some things are open to discussion. Others are not. The Disqualification Move is the tactic of explicitly removing one thing from the conversation in a way that reshapes what the conversation becomes.

A reader named Jack had been in a five-month dating situation that was not moving forward. The woman he was seeing wanted to keep things casual. He wanted more. Every time he brought up the definition of the relationship, she would deflect. Every direct ask had made him feel like he was chasing.

I told him to try a Disqualification Move. Not a breakup. Not an ultimatum. A removal of a specific thing from the conversation.

Jack said to her, in a neutral moment: "I want to say one thing. I have realized I am not going to have the 'what are we' conversation with you anymore. It is not working for either of us. If the shape of this is going to change, it will change because one of us wants it to, not because we talked about it. I am going to stop asking."

He did not say he was breaking up with her. He did not say he wanted more. He removed a specific topic from the conversational table.

She reached out the next day asking to get dinner. At dinner she said she had been thinking about what he said and she wanted to define the relationship. He had not asked. The Disqualification Move had removed the thing she had been defending against, and in its absence, she reached for it herself.

How to deploy. When a specific topic in a relationship has become unproductive, remove it explicitly. Not as a threat. As a structural decision. *I am not going to discuss X anymore* is the basic shape. The topic being removed should be one that the other person has been benefiting from discussing repeatedly.

The failure mode is using this as veiled coercion. If the disqualification is actually a pressure tactic, the listener hears it as such and the detector fires. The removal must be genuine. You are not holding the topic hostage. You are setting it down.

Four tactics. Four ways to change the dynamic underneath a conversation when the surface-level tactics have not worked. Each one shifts something structural. Who is pursuing, who is allied with whom, whose emotion is driving, what can be discussed. Use them with care. These are the tactics that do not come back.

*Field note: some tactics work by adding. The best ones work by removing something nobody even knew was there.*
