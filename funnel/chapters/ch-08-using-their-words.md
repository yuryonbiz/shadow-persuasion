# Chapter 8

## Using Their Words Against Their Resistance

The fastest way to get someone to agree with a position is to have them agree with themselves. You cannot argue with your own words. You can only restate them, clarify them, or abandon them entirely. All three of those work in your favor when you are trying to move a conversation.

The four tactics in this chapter use the listener's own statements, values, feelings, and commitments against their own resistance. In each, the thing that produces agreement is something the listener said, not something you said.

### Tactic #17: The Earlier Agreement

Robert Cialdini, in *Influence*, identifies commitment and consistency as one of his six principles. People who have publicly stated a position feel pressure to remain consistent with it. Most readers interpret this as a tactic about getting small commitments early, then escalating. That is the visible version, and sophisticated listeners now spot it coming.

The Earlier Agreement is the stealth version. You do not ask for a new commitment. You reference an existing one. Something the other person already said, believed, or stated as a principle, earlier in this conversation or in the past. When you do, they are no longer disagreeing with you. They are disagreeing with themselves. And people rarely disagree with themselves out loud.

In 2018, the year before Dana, I was having a conversation with my manager about a raise. He had just told me the number was not going to work this cycle. I had been there before and knew where the conversation typically went. Usually, at this point, I would argue my case on metrics. Quarterly numbers. Specific deals. Client retention. And he would nod and say something about the budget.

Instead I said: "Six months ago, you told me that the team we wanted to build here was people who would stay for five or more years, and that the way you retained people was making sure they felt paid fairly before they started asking. Do you still think that is true?"

He said yes.

I said: "I am asking before I start asking."

He looked at me for a second. Said he would look into it. Two weeks later he came back with the number I had asked for. Not because I had argued the metrics. Because I had put him in a position where denying the raise meant contradicting something he had said six months earlier. Consistency with his own stated principle was stronger than the budget pressure that had made him say no five minutes into our meeting.

How to deploy. Before any conversation that matters, remember specific things the other person has said, believed, or publicly committed to. When resistance comes up, reference one of those earlier statements, neutrally, as a question. Not as a gotcha. You are giving them the chance to be consistent with themselves.

The failure mode is framing it as a gotcha. *You literally said six months ago that...* fires the detector immediately and produces defensive backpedaling. The reference has to be curious and even-handed, as if you are just checking whether their position has changed.

### Tactic #18: The Named Feeling

Voss's labels work when they are accurate. When they are not, they fire the detector in under a second. The label is perhaps the most visible tactic in the entire modern persuasion playbook, and by now it is the one sophisticated listeners spot fastest.

The Named Feeling keeps the mechanism but inverts the purpose. Instead of labeling to produce rapport, you label to produce correction. You name a feeling the other person is having, knowing there is a decent chance you will be wrong. If you are right, they feel seen. If you are wrong, they correct you, and the correction reveals what is actually going on. Either outcome is progress. You cannot lose from an honestly-delivered Named Feeling, as long as you are not trying to land a specific emotion for manipulation purposes.

A reader wrote to me about a conversation she had been dreading with her mother. Her mother had a pattern of making small cutting comments over the phone, dressed as concern. "You sound tired. Are you eating well? I worry about you." The comments had a way of making my reader feel judged and controlled. Direct confrontation had produced years of denial and counter-attack. She was going into another call and bracing for the pattern.

I suggested she try a Named Feeling the next time her mother said one. Something real, delivered without aggression.

Her mother said, about twenty minutes into the call: "You look thin in that photo. Are you okay?"

My reader said: "I get the sense that when you say things like that, you are worried about me. Is that what is going on, or is something else happening?"

Her mother was quiet for a long moment. Then said: "I think I just miss you."

The conversation changed completely. My reader later told me that in thirty-seven years, her mother had never said she missed her out loud. The small cutting comments had been, it turned out, the only way her mother knew how to express longing. Naming a possible feeling gave her mother the chance to correct the label, and the correction was the first honest statement her mother had made in the relationship in years.

How to deploy. When someone is behaving in a way you do not understand, name the possible feeling underneath it, as a guess you might be wrong about. Frame it as curiosity, not diagnosis. If you are right, the relationship moves forward. If you are wrong, you just got more information.

The failure mode is labeling with certainty. *You are clearly feeling X* puts the other person in a position of having to argue with your interpretation of them. That fires the detector harder than saying nothing. The Named Feeling is always a question, never a statement.

### Tactic #19: The Pre-Understood Objection

In any conversation where you want someone to agree to something, there is a short list of objections they are going to raise. You know what they are. They know what they are. The fastest way to lose a conversation is to pitch through all of them as if they do not exist. The fastest way to win is to name them first.

The Pre-Understood Objection is the move of stating the listener's likely objection before they can raise it, and addressing it before they have to. Done poorly, this looks like defensive preempting. Done well, it creates the experience of being understood.

A sales rep I coached named Marcus was trying to close a $180,000 deal with a prospect who kept going quiet between meetings. Three emails, two weeks of radio silence, then a scheduled call. He called me the day before the call for advice.

I asked him what the prospect's three likeliest objections were. He listed them immediately. He had been thinking about them for two weeks. Price was one, and I told him to leave that alone. The other two were more interesting: the prospect was worried the solution would not integrate with an existing legacy tool, and was worried that the implementation timeline would land during a product launch his team was running in Q2.

On the call, Marcus opened with: "Before we get into the deck, I want to say two things out loud that I think are probably the real reasons this has been slower than either of us wanted. I think you are worried that the integration with your Salesforce instance is going to be a nightmare because the last vendor you tried this with took six months. And I think the timing right now is bad for you because of the April launch. If those are wrong, tell me and we can move on. If they are right, I want to talk about them first."

The prospect was silent for about eight seconds. Then said: "It is like you have been inside my head."

Marcus closed the deal on that call.

How to deploy. Before any important conversation, list the three objections the other person is most likely to raise. Pick the one or two that are the real blockers, not the surface ones. State them yourself, out loud, before the other person does. Then address them.

The failure mode is naming a decoy objection. If you state the objection you are most comfortable rebutting rather than the real one, the detector catches it instantly. The listener knows which concern is actually theirs, and they know when you are dodging it. Name the one that scares you most to name. That one is the real one.

### Tactic #20: The Small Yes That Isn't

The classic "foot in the door" technique from mid-century persuasion research involves getting a small agreement first and escalating to the real ask. The modern version has been so widely taught that any sophisticated listener spots it in the second exchange.

The Small Yes That Isn't keeps the commitment mechanism but hides the trajectory. You get a micro-agreement on something that seems unrelated to your actual ask. The agreement is small, specific, and appears to have no downstream consequence. It primes the listener's sense of cooperation without triggering the detector, because they cannot see what the small yes is setting up.

A reader named Leah was in a long-running pattern with her mother-in-law, who had, over several years, made a habit of giving Leah and her husband unsolicited parenting advice that Leah found both condescending and damaging to her confidence as a new mother. Direct pushback had caused years of family tension.

I suggested a Small Yes in a low-stakes moment. Leah was at dinner at her in-laws' house. Her mother-in-law was about to start what Leah could feel was going to be another advice session. Leah said: "Before you start, can I ask you something? Do you think I am a good listener?"

Her mother-in-law, caught slightly off-guard, said yes, of course.

Leah said: "Thank you. I try to be. And because I try to be, when I do not take every suggestion you make, it is not because I did not hear it. It is usually because I have already thought about it and decided to do something different. Would that be okay to trust, going forward, even when it is hard?"

Her mother-in-law said yes again, more slowly.

The advice sessions did not end completely. But they reduced significantly. And when they did happen, Leah's declining to follow the advice no longer carried the implication of disrespect. Her mother-in-law had agreed, in advance and in her own words, that declining was not a signal that the advice had not been heard.

How to deploy. Before the conversation where the real agreement is needed, set up a small yes on an adjacent principle. The small yes should feel incidental and true. The connection to the real ask should be invisible until later.

The failure mode is stacking small yeses too obviously. If the listener catches the pattern of escalating agreements, the whole chain collapses. Use at most one Small Yes per conversation, and make it genuinely small.

These four tactics share one structure. Each uses the listener's own words, principles, or feelings as the source of the agreement. You are not arguing. You are surfacing agreements that already existed and letting them do the work.

*Field note: no one argues with themselves out loud. If you can get the conversation to a place where they are doing that, the rest happens on its own.*
