# Chapter 14

## Commitment Without Asking For It

The tactics in this chapter deepen a commitment after it has been made, without any of the moves that traditional sales and negotiation training prescribe. Every one of them makes the agreement more durable. None of them ask for more than what was already given.

This is counterintuitive in a way the rest of the book should have trained you for by now. The instinct, once you have a yes, is to do something to it. Sign a contract. Announce it. Celebrate it. Set a deadline. Those moves deepen nothing. They just remind the other person what they just agreed to, which is the last thing you want.

The four tactics here produce depth without attention.

### Tactic #40: The Public Witness

When someone makes a commitment privately, they can unmake it privately. When someone makes the same commitment in front of a third party, the psychological cost of unmaking it increases significantly. Not because the third party will enforce anything. Because human memory for our own past statements is unreliable, but our memory for what we said in front of others, and their memory of what we said, is much sharper.

The Public Witness is the move of introducing, into the post-agreement conversation, a third party who now knows about the commitment. You do this without making it feel like a commitment-locking move. It can be a casual mention. A name dropped. A calendar invite with a third person cc'd. The mechanism is automatic once the third party is in the loop.

A reader named Maya was a team lead at a marketing agency. She had been working for six months on a project she thought deserved a raise. After a long one-on-one with her VP, her VP had agreed verbally that a raise was appropriate and would come through in the next compensation cycle. Maya had heard similar promises before. Sometimes they happened. Sometimes they quietly did not.

I suggested a Public Witness move. Not through HR. Through a casual introduction of the promise into a three-way conversation.

Two days after the one-on-one, at the end of an unrelated team meeting with her VP and the Head of People, Maya said: "Before we wrap, a small thing. Patricia, the VP and I talked on Tuesday about a raise for me that should come through in the next cycle. Nothing needed now. Just wanted to loop you in since it will probably touch your calendar."

Patricia acknowledged. The VP nodded. The meeting ended.

The raise came through the following cycle without any additional pressure or follow-up. Maya had not asked for anything. She had simply transferred the private commitment into a shared awareness. The raise no longer depended on the VP remembering to execute. It was now a thing Patricia was waiting to see execute, which changed the structural cost of it not happening.

How to deploy. After a private commitment, introduce the commitment into a conversation where a relevant third party is present. Do it casually. Make it a small logistical mention, not a commitment check.

The failure mode is making the Public Witness move feel like an enforcement mechanism. If the other person experiences it as you locking them in, they feel cornered, and the detector fires. The move has to feel incidental, almost like a calendar-hygiene note.

### Tactic #41: The One-Line Email

When a verbal agreement is followed by a long written confirmation, the length of the confirmation becomes its own risk. Every paragraph in a confirmation email gives the recipient another chance to re-read the agreement and second-guess it. Long confirmations reopen the negotiation.

The One-Line Email is the move of following a verbal agreement with a written message so short that there is no surface for second-guessing to land on. Twelve words. Maybe fifteen. The agreement is acknowledged, the next step is indicated, and the message is closed.

A sales leader I coached named Teresa was running an enterprise sales team with a 31% reversal rate on verbal agreements. Meaning almost a third of deals that had been verbally agreed to were dropping out before paperwork landed. The team had been writing thorough post-agreement emails summarizing the terms, the implementation timeline, the kickoff process, the success metrics, and next steps. Standard enterprise sales practice.

I told her to have the team cut the emails to one sentence. Reversal rate dropped from 31% to 4% in three months.

The new email looked like this: "Thanks for the call, Marcus. Sending the paperwork over Friday. Call you Monday."

That is it. No summary. No terms. No kickoff plan. Nothing that gives the recipient a chance to re-read the agreement and find something to object to. The relationship stays in forward motion instead of looping back through a written artifact.

How to deploy. After a verbal agreement, send one email within twenty-four hours. Keep it under fifteen words. Acknowledge. Indicate next step. Close. Save the details for a later message after the commitment has stabilized.

The failure mode is adding one more sentence. The temptation is enormous. You want to re-confirm the terms, or mention one clarifying point, or summarize what was agreed. Do not. Any sentence beyond the minimum reopens the negotiation. One sentence is enough.

### Tactic #42: The Self-Referral

People rarely advocate for something unless they feel ownership over the idea of it. When you ask someone to recommend you, to introduce you, or to pass on your work, their brain frames it as a favor to you, which means they weigh it and often decline. When the same person reaches that thought on their own, the framing is completely different. They are acting on their own preference.

The Self-Referral is the move of asking someone, at the right moment, who else might benefit from what you just talked about. Not who would be a good referral for you. Who, in their network, would benefit.

A reader named Elena was a consultant who built a consulting practice almost entirely on referrals, with no outbound marketing. Her approach was simple and she mentioned it to me in passing on a call.

At the end of every client engagement, when the work had gone well, she would ask the client a specific question: "Thinking about what we just did, is there anyone in your network who you think would benefit from a version of this?"

The question does two things. First, it frames the referral as a favor to a third party, not to Elena. The client's brain does not process it as "do something for Elena." It processes it as "help someone you care about by connecting them with something useful." Second, it makes the client the agent. They are the one deciding who benefits. The referral, when it comes, has their enthusiasm attached.

Elena's closure rate on referrals generated this way is roughly triple what she gets from direct outreach. She does not ask her clients to refer her. She asks her clients to think about who else would benefit from the work.

How to deploy. At the natural end of a successful engagement, meeting, or interaction, ask the other person who in their network would benefit from what just happened. Frame the question as their opportunity to help a third party, not your opportunity to get a referral.

The failure mode is asking for a referral instead of asking about benefit. *Do you know anyone who might need my services* is a visible referral-ask and the detector fires on it. *Who in your network would benefit from what we just did* is a different question structurally, even though it generates the same outcome.

### Tactic #43: The Minimum Viable Commitment

After a verbal agreement, the temptation is to ask for the biggest possible first step that confirms the commitment. A signed contract. A deposit. A formal kickoff call. Those are visible commitment-locking moves and they often backfire, because the step is large enough that scheduling it alone becomes a point of potential reversal.

The Minimum Viable Commitment is the smallest step that converts the agreement into forward motion. A fifteen-minute calendar hold. A two-line email. A single introduction. The step should be so small that saying no to it would feel disproportionate to what was just agreed to.

A reader named Dana (different Dana) was dealing with her elderly father's resistance to a medical recommendation. He had agreed verbally to see a specialist. He had been "agreeing" to see a specialist for eight months. Nothing had happened.

The family had been pushing him to commit to a specific appointment. He had been stalling. The gap between his verbal agreement and any actual step was the problem.

I suggested Dana ask for something much smaller. Not the appointment. The introduction.

She asked him: "Would you be okay if I called Dr. Martinez's office Monday and asked them to tell me what their intake process looks like? I will find out what the wait time is, what he needs ahead of the first visit, and I will let you know. You can decide from there."

Her father said yes.

She called Monday. The intake process was straightforward. She reported back. He said okay, book it. The appointment happened three weeks later. The chain of commitment had gone from agreement to action in two small moves that did not feel like commitment-locking.

How to deploy. After a verbal agreement, identify the smallest possible step that converts the commitment into motion. Not the next big step. The smallest next step. Ask for that. The larger steps will happen through momentum after the small one.

The failure mode is asking for the logical next step instead of the smallest one. The logical next step is usually too big. It gives the other person a visible commitment to refuse. The smallest step is harder to refuse and produces the same ultimate trajectory.

Four tactics. Four ways to deepen a commitment without asking for more than what was given. Each one does its work after the agreement has been reached, in the brief window where the agreement is still crystallizing into something durable.

*Field note: write the email they would write to themselves in three hours if they were very busy. That is the email you want to send.*
