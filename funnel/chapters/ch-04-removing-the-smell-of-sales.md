# Chapter 4

## Removing the Smell of Sales

The last chapter covered the first thirty seconds. This one covers what happens in the next thirty minutes.

The detector does not fully disarm in the opening. It stays tuned throughout the conversation to whether you are someone who is here to sell, pitch, or convince. Every word you say in the first half of any important exchange is being quietly evaluated against one question: *does this person smell like sales?*

The three tactics in this chapter are about making the answer no. Not by suppressing yourself. By doing different work than the work salespeople do.

### Tactic #5: The Consultant Stance

The default posture in almost every high-stakes conversation is promotional. You present a solution before you have fully heard the problem. You explain your position before you have understood theirs. You pitch. Even when you think you are listening, your internal monologue is preparing your response.

The listener's detector catches this posture instantly. They can feel when someone is waiting for their turn to speak versus when someone is actually diagnosing their situation. Genuine diagnosis is rare enough in adult life that it creates trust on contact.

The Consultant Stance is the move of explicitly positioning yourself as diagnostic before promotional. You ask what is actually wrong. You ask what they have already tried. You ask what the constraint really is. You do this not because it is a "consultative selling" technique you read about in a sales book (Voss and a dozen others teach versions of it), but because until you have diagnosed, you have nothing worth saying anyway.

A reader wrote to me about a coworker I will call Derek, who had spent two years subtly undermining her. Taking credit for her work in meetings. Leaving her off emails where she should have been copied. Making comments in the team Slack channel that were just ambiguous enough to be deniable and just pointed enough to set her up.

Every direct approach had failed. Asking him to stop had produced *I didn't mean it that way.* Confronting him privately had produced *that's a misunderstanding.* Escalating to their manager had produced a forty-minute HR conversation and no behavior change.

I told her to stop trying to correct him. Next time he did it, to ask a diagnostic question in public where everyone could see. Not accusatory. Not suspicious. Genuinely curious.

A week later, he posted a passive-aggressive comment in a team channel suggesting she had not prepared for an upcoming meeting. She replied, publicly: "I've noticed a pattern over the last few months where you send messages like this one in public channels. I'm curious what's driving it. Is there something about how I'm showing up that's creating friction for you?"

He did not respond for two hours. Then he direct-messaged her saying he had been stressed and did not mean anything by it. The pattern stopped that week. It has not returned in the eight months since.

The Consultant Stance neutralized a pattern that direct confrontation had failed to stop for two years. The reason: there is no counter-move in the manipulator's playbook to a genuine diagnostic question. Accusations can be denied. Observations are harder. Diagnostic questions land somewhere in between, and the manipulator has no rehearsed response for them.

How to deploy. In any conversation where you could naturally pitch, opine, or correct, pause. Ask a diagnostic question first. *What's the underlying thing you're trying to solve?* Or *what's been getting in the way?* Or *what would useful look like to you?* Listen to the answer. Only after they have exhausted their diagnostic content do you offer anything of your own.

The failure mode is making the question sound like a sales script. "What are your biggest pain points?" is not a diagnostic question. It is an SDR opener. Real diagnostic questions are specific to the person in front of you, not generic consulting-speak that could be asked of anyone.

### Tactic #6: The Confession First

In 1996, a social psychologist named Steven Fein ran a series of experiments on what happens when listeners are told in advance that a speaker might be biased. The intuitive prediction was that the warning would make listeners more skeptical. The actual result was the opposite. Listeners who had been told about the potential bias became *less* resistant to the speaker's message, not more.

The mechanism is counterintuitive but clean. Once the potential bias has been named out loud by the speaker, the listener's detector stops looking for it. The job of catching the move is complete. The message can now be evaluated on its merits, because the thing the listener was primed to watch for has already been acknowledged.

This is the Confession First. You open a conversation by admitting a weakness, bias, or thing the listener might already suspect. You name it before they have to. Done well, it makes the detector downshift. Done poorly, it sounds like false humility and fires the detector harder.

A founder I coached named Mark was raising a $500K seed round in early 2023. His first three investor meetings that week had all ended with vague "let me think about it" signals, which every founder knows is investor code for no.

In the debrief I asked him to walk me through his opener. It was the standard founder pitch opener. *We are disrupting [industry] by using AI to solve [problem] for [customer segment].* I had heard the same opener from four other founders that month. So had every investor he was meeting with.

I told him to throw the opener out entirely. The next pitch, I gave him this to open with instead: "I'll save us both time. There are three things wrong with my pitch that you are going to notice in the next fifteen minutes. Let me name them first so we don't have to dance around them."

Then he listed three real weaknesses. Burn rate was too high for a seed company. The team was missing a key engineering hire. The addressable market was smaller than the deck made it look. Real weaknesses, named specifically, with no softballing.

Then he walked through why he thought it worked anyway.

The investor signed the next day. $500K at 4% dilution. Terms Mark had not been asking for.

What happened inside the investor's head: the detector had been primed by three months of founder pitches that all claimed to be disruptive, dominant, and inevitable. Mark's opener did not sound like any of them. His detector downshifted. By the time the real pitch landed, the investor was listening in a mode investors rarely listen in, which is openly.

How to deploy. In any pitch, negotiation, or important relational conversation, open with a specific confession about what is genuinely wrong or what you have gotten wrong. Not a softballed humility move. *I'm not sure if I'm qualified to...* is not a confession. It is pre-emptive apologizing. A real confession is specific, named, and the kind of thing the listener would have found out on their own in the next ten minutes anyway.

The failure mode is fake confession. Vague statements like *I don't have all the answers* or *I'm not perfect* register immediately as a move. They actually make the detector fire harder, because now the listener suspects you are running the confession tactic. Real confessions have specific content.

### Tactic #7: The False Concession

When someone walks into a conversation expecting pushback on a specific point, their brain has pre-loaded their argument for that point. They are waiting for you to resist. When you agree instead, the pre-loaded argument has nowhere to go. It dissipates. The whole rhetorical frame they were operating in collapses. They have to start over, off-script, and what comes out next is almost always closer to the real issue.

The False Concession is the move of agreeing with a point the other person thought you would fight. The word "false" refers to the expectation, not the concession itself. The concession is real, but the expectation that you would resist it was false.

A coaching client I will call Tom was fighting with his sixteen-year-old daughter about her Friday-night curfew. Every week, same argument. She wanted 1am. He wanted 11pm. The fight had been running for six months and was poisoning the rest of the relationship.

I asked him what her argument was. He quoted her: *All my friends get to stay out until 1am. You're not being fair.*

I told him, next Friday when she said it, to agree with her. Fully. And then stop talking.

He did it that Friday. He said: "You're right. It's not fair that your friends get to stay out later than you. I get why that's frustrating."

Then he said nothing.

Tom described the next four seconds to me as the longest four seconds of his year. His daughter had three more sentences pre-loaded and ready to fire. They all assumed he would push back. They had nowhere to go. She stood in the kitchen for those four seconds and then said, "well... yeah, it's really frustrating."

Now the conversation was actually about the frustration, not the curfew. They talked for twenty minutes about her specific friend group, a social dynamic she was worried about, and two girls she had been trying to distance herself from. When they were done, she said she would be home by midnight. On her own. He had not negotiated her down from 1am. She had walked the request back herself, because the curfew was never the real issue.

The argument about "fairness" was a placeholder. She did not know how to start the real conversation, so she had been using the curfew as a stand-in for six months. When Tom agreed with the placeholder, she had to surface what was actually going on.

How to deploy. When someone presents an argument they expect you to resist, listen for the weakest version of their claim that you could honestly agree with. Agree with that version. Then stop talking. The stand-in argument evaporates and the real conversation begins.

The failure mode is faking the concession. If you agree insincerely, the listener can hear it and reads it as a move. The concession has to be something you actually believe is true, even if it is only one narrow part of their broader position. A false concession is worse than argument. It reads as manipulation, and the detector fires harder than it would have in a normal disagreement.

These three tactics share one property. They do not look like sales. That is the design.

The best salespeople, the best negotiators, the best parents in the toughest conversations of their lives, do not look like they are running anything. They look like they are paying attention and telling the truth. The real work is underneath.

There is a line often attributed to the advertising legend David Ogilvy: *nothing kills a sale like trying to make one.* I have never been able to find the original source. It may be apocryphal. Either way, it is the right line for the chapter.

*Field note: the best salespeople do not look like salespeople. Once you see this, you cannot unsee who is actually selling to you.*
