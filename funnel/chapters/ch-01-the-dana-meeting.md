# Chapter 1

## The Dana Meeting

It's March 12th, 2019. The elevator hits the 47th floor of a building on Park Avenue and I step out onto a carpet so thick my shoes sink.

I'm here to close an $847,000 deal with a woman I've been trying to reach for six months. Her name is Dana Ruiz. She's the CFO of a logistics company I'm not allowed to name in this book. She's fifty-four, she's been in finance since 1989, and three different people have told me she doesn't buy from vendors she doesn't like.

I've read Chris Voss's *Never Split the Difference* twice. Both times marked up. I've drilled labels and calibrated questions with a partner in my company's training room until I could do them in my sleep. I have a one-page strategy doc folded into my blazer. Fourteen specific moves, mapped to the three objection paths my manager and I think are most likely.

I'm ready.

She walks into the conference room seven minutes late, drops her phone on the table face-down, and says, "You have forty minutes."

---

I open with a mirror. Low-stakes. Warm.

"Forty minutes."

She glances up. "Forty minutes."

That's the move. Get them to repeat themselves. Anchor the time frame. Build a rhythm.

I label. "It sounds like your day has been intense."

She doesn't answer. She opens a bottle of water.

I try a calibrated question. "How are you thinking about the renewal conversation?"

"I'm here," she says. "Talk."

I notice my palms are damp. That's fine. That's normal.

I start my pitch. Softly. Structured. I hit the reciprocity opener — a compliment I pulled from a public op-ed she wrote in *CFO* magazine two years earlier. "The line in your piece about compound interest on organizational decisions changed how I think about what we do."

She nods. Once.

I deploy the no-oriented question. "Is it crazy to think we could cut the implementation to six weeks instead of twelve?"

She looks at me for a second longer than she should.

I label again, carefully. "It seems like you've been here before. Vendors promising six weeks and delivering eighteen."

She tilts her head, maybe a quarter of an inch.

I can see I'm losing her.

So I do what every sales book ever written tells you to do when you're losing them. I lean in. I stack. I run the "that's right" gambit — summarizing her position back to her to land an emotional alignment. I mirror the last three words of every sentence she offers.

Thirty-two minutes in, Dana holds up one finger.

"Nate."

I stop.

"I can hear you running the Voss playbook on me."

The room stops.

"I've read it too. Let's just talk."

---

What I want you to understand about this moment is that it wasn't the loss that wrecked me.

I'd lost deals before. I'd lose them again.

It was that I could hear myself from the other side of the table for the first time. I heard the labels as labels. The mirrors as mirrors. The calibrated questions as what they were — moves. A man running moves on another person who was paying attention.

My stomach dropped somewhere I didn't know my stomach could go.

I tried to pivot. I said something about how I was just trying to make sure I understood her position. I said I appreciated her directness. I asked if we could start over.

We could not start over.

The rest of the meeting was slack rope. I pitched the product. She asked three questions. I answered. She thanked me for my time.

On the elevator down to the lobby I looked at my phone. The screen was black. I hadn't gotten a notification since I'd walked in. For forty minutes, nothing in the outside world had happened. Only this.

I'd done everything right.

I'd done everything right and she'd read me like a magazine.

---

The deal went to the incumbent eleven days later.

Worse, Dana mentioned the meeting to a contact on my company's board. The phrase that got back to my VP was *transactional and performative*. He used it in my performance review six weeks later without realizing he was quoting her through two hops of hearsay.

He offered me a demotion to mid-market.

I quit in the meeting.

I had nine months of runway, a wife who worked freelance and mostly out-earned me, and no clear plan. Eleven years of selling enterprise software. My only skill was the one that had just broken.

---

For about a year after Dana, I read.

Every sales book I owned, which was most of them. Every influence book, every persuasion book. The whole shelf. I read them slower than the first time. I took notes in the margins. I was looking for what I'd missed.

I found nothing.

Every one of those books was telling me the same story I'd just lost a deal executing. Anchor. Mirror. Label. Build rapport. Close. The same moves. The same shelf. It had taught me to do exactly what Dana had called out.

The book that finally cracked it open wasn't on that shelf.

I was re-reading *Thinking, Fast and Slow* for the third time. It's long. Not tactical. I wasn't reading it to solve anything. I liked the way Kahneman thought.

Somewhere in the middle of the book, in a footnote I'd never paid attention to, there's a reference to a 1966 paper by a psychologist named Jack Brehm. The paper is called *A Theory of Psychological Reactance*.

I Google Scholared it at 11 PM on a Tuesday.

The abstract described something I'd experienced my whole adult life without knowing had a name. Brehm had proposed — with data — that when humans perceive an attempt to influence them, their immediate response isn't assessment. It's resistance. Not resistance to the content of the request. Resistance to being influenced, full stop.

I read the paper that night. Then every paper in its citation list. Then the follow-up literature — Miron and Brehm 2006, Dillard and Shen 2005, a meta-analysis that pulled together sixty years of results.

And I understood Dana.

---

The entire mainstream persuasion industry — everything I had ever paid for, studied, or practiced — was a manual for firing the reactance alarm.

That was what it was.

Cialdini's principles are visible. Voss's labels are visible. Greene's laws are visible. Carnegie's smiles are visible. A skilled listener sees a labeled emotion land and the first thing they feel isn't validation. They feel *this person is running something on me*.

The best-selling persuasion books on earth were teaching people to fire the very alarm they were trying to bypass.

It seemed too simple to be true. It was also obviously true the moment I saw it.

I'd been deploying a playbook against someone who had the playbook memorized better than I did. It hadn't mattered that I'd executed it well. The execution was the problem.

---

So I did something that took me another fourteen months and that I don't recommend casually.

I started recording every conversation I had that mattered.

Where I live, recording a conversation is legal with one-party consent. That was me. I wasn't planning to publish anything. I just needed data.

I recorded every sales call I took in my consulting gigs. Then I started recording negotiations. Then — with consent — therapy sessions. A couple of marriage fights my wife agreed to after I explained what I was doing. My son's parent-teacher conferences. A long, ugly argument with a contractor about a kitchen renovation.

Four hundred and seven hours.

I transcribed the ones where I won. Transcribed the ones where I lost. Tagged every technique I used — every label, every calibrated question, every story, every silence. Tagged them by timestamp. Built a spreadsheet I still have and still update.

What emerged wasn't what I expected.

The wins didn't correlate with the tactics I had added to the conversation. They correlated with tactics I had removed.

The losses didn't correlate with being unprepared. They correlated with being *prepared and visible*.

In the transcripts of my best performances, I could find almost nothing recognizable from any sales book. I wasn't labeling. I wasn't mirroring. I wasn't anchoring. I was doing something else. Something quieter and harder to name at first.

Forty-seven of those something-elses eventually became this book.

---

A friend of a friend offered me a VP of Sales job at a Series B company in late 2022. Not what I was looking for. But runway was runway, and the founder told me I could run the team however I wanted.

I spent the first month teaching the team to remove things.

Shorter emails. Cut qualifying questions from seven to two. Stop naming emotions unless you were absolutely certain what emotion was there. Stop trying to *build rapport* and just be useful. Stop closing — let the conversation arrive at its own ending.

Close rate went from 18% to 41% over eleven months. Revenue 3.4x. I was a VP at forty, which is what I'd been telling myself I wanted to be.

I quit a year later.

It wasn't the work. The work was fine. It was that I had a thing I believed in, and hitting quota for a Series B wasn't the way to spread it.

I took on three private coaching clients at a price that made them serious. Then seven. Then a waitlist of forty. I have a rule I keep: never more than seven clients at a time. The thing I'm teaching requires attention. I can't give attention to thirty-five people.

The waitlist is why you're reading this.

This book is the only way I could figure out to help more than seven people a year.

---

Two things before we go any further.

The first is that almost everything you're about to read will feel wrong the first time you read it. That's the design. The tactics in this book are counterintuitive by definition — if they were intuitive, everyone would already be doing them, and they wouldn't work. Don't trust the ones that sound obviously right. Pay attention to the ones that make you pause.

The second is that none of this is new.

Brehm wrote his paper in 1966. Cialdini published *Influence* in 1984. Voss published *Never Split the Difference* in 2016. What I'm offering you isn't new research. It's a re-reading — a way of looking at the same sixty years of data from the angle nobody looks from.

The shelf of influence books everyone has read is a shelf of offensive tactics. What happens when you read it as a shelf of mistakes?

You get this book.

*Field note: a month after the Dana meeting, she referred me to her successor at a different company. I closed him in one call, using the same mistake — inverted. You can use failure like scaffolding, once you understand what broke.*
