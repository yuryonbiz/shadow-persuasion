# Chapter 7

## Stories Beat Pitches

Humans are built to resist pitches. We are not built to resist stories.

The three tactics in this chapter exploit the difference. A pitch announces itself and fires the detector. A story bypasses the detector entirely, because the listener does not yet know whether the story has a point. By the time they realize it does, they have already done the work of identifying with the protagonist, feeling the decision, and reaching the conclusion themselves.

A useful rule for the rest of your life: if you find yourself about to argue, tell a story instead.

### Tactic #14: The Borrowed Conclusion

When you tell someone what to think, their detector fires. When you tell them a story about a third party who reached a particular conclusion, they reach their own conclusion inside the story. That conclusion, reached privately, is the one they will defend later as their own idea.

The Borrowed Conclusion is the move of giving the listener the conclusion you want them to reach, but placing it inside another person's situation. You narrate. You do not argue. The listener does the work.

Researchers Melanie Green and Timothy Brock at Ohio State published a paper in 2000 called *The Role of Transportation in the Persuasiveness of Public Narratives*. Their finding, which has been replicated many times since, was that listeners who get absorbed in a story show significantly lower levels of counter-argument than listeners who receive the same information as a direct argument. When people are transported into a narrative, the critical filter that normally rejects incoming claims goes quiet.

A coaching client of mine named Derek was a senior engineer at a Series C software company. He had been trying, for seven months, to convince his VP of Engineering to shut down a failing product line that was consuming a third of the team's cycles. Every meeting in which he made the case directly had ended with the VP defending the line and reminding Derek of the sunk cost.

I told him to stop pitching. Next time the topic came up, he would tell a story instead.

The next week in a staff meeting, the VP mentioned the product line again. Derek waited for an opening and said: "I was reading about what happened at Dropbox around 2015. They had Mailbox, the email client they had acquired. Great team. Technically impressive. Drain on the core business. At some point the CEO decided they could either keep investing and hope it paid off, or shut it down and redirect the team. They shut it down. A year later their core product was back on track. The team that had been on Mailbox became the team that built Paper."

Derek said nothing else about the topic. Did not connect it to their situation. Did not make eye contact with the VP when he said it.

Two weeks later, in a different meeting, the VP said: "I was thinking about that Mailbox thing. I think we need to have a conversation about the product line." Six weeks after that, the product line was sunset. Derek's team moved to the core product. The VP, to this day, describes the decision as his own. Derek does not correct him.

How to deploy. When you want someone to reach a specific conclusion, do not present arguments. Find a real story about another person or company that reached that conclusion under similar circumstances. Tell the story neutrally. Do not draw the parallel for them. Let them do it on their own time.

The failure mode is closing the story with a punchline. The moment you say *and that is exactly what we should do here*, you have collapsed the story back into a pitch, and the detector fires retroactively. The story must end without a connection to the listener's situation. The connection is for them to make.

### Tactic #15: The Third Party Story

The Borrowed Conclusion is about importing an outcome from elsewhere. The Third Party Story is about importing a *person* the listener can identify with. Structurally, both involve narrative. The difference is that the Third Party Story is told from the perspective of a character the listener sees themselves in. They become the protagonist in their own head while you are telling it.

A coaching client of mine named Liz came to me because her fifteen-year-old daughter had spent the past year drifting deeper into a friendship that Liz was sure was bad for her. The girl, call her Jenna, was a year older, had recently been in trouble for shoplifting, and seemed to be pulling Liz's daughter in a direction that scared Liz. Every direct conversation had ended in Liz's daughter shutting down and defending Jenna harder.

I asked Liz whether there was anyone in her own life she had cut off as a teenager, and why. She had one. A close friend from age fourteen to sixteen who had been pulling her in directions she eventually realized she did not want to go. She had ended the friendship painfully.

I told her to tell her daughter that story. Not as a lesson. As a story. Over dinner, in a casual moment, when the daughter was not braced.

Liz told me later that she told the story almost exactly as I had suggested it. Named her friend. Described what she had liked about her. Described the first time she had realized something was off. Described how long it had taken her to end it. Did not mention Jenna. Did not mention her daughter. Did not draw a conclusion.

Three weeks later, her daughter stopped spending time with Jenna. She did not say anything to Liz about it. Liz did not ask. Five months have passed. The friendship has not resumed. Liz's daughter is closer to her now than she has been in two years, which Liz attributes less to the broken friendship than to the fact that her daughter realized her mother had told her something real.

How to deploy. When you want someone to see their situation clearly, find a real experience of your own, or of someone the listener knows and trusts, that maps onto the situation the listener is in. Tell the story in the first person or in the third person about someone specific. Do not make the parallel explicit. Do not tell them what to learn from it.

The failure mode is obvious parallel. If the story maps too cleanly onto the listener's current situation, they see what you are doing immediately. The story should be close enough to trigger identification but not so close that the listener feels targeted.

### Tactic #16: The Preview

When you describe a future moment to someone in enough sensory detail, their brain treats that future moment as if it had already happened. This is well-documented in cognitive research: the brain does not distinguish cleanly between vividly imagined experiences and actual memories. A richly described future event gets filed in the same mental cabinet as things that really happened.

The Preview is the move of describing, in specific sensory detail, the moment the listener will experience on the other side of the decision they are considering. Not the outcome abstractly. The actual moment, with specifics they can see and feel. Once that moment is installed, reversing the decision feels like erasing something that already happened.

A reader of mine, a man named Caleb, had a younger brother who had been struggling with alcohol for four years. Multiple failed attempts at quitting. The family had tried every form of intervention. Everyone had made every argument. The conversations ended in fights and the brother kept drinking.

I coached Caleb to try something different. No arguments. No statistics. No ultimatums. One conversation in which he would describe a specific future moment.

Caleb sat down with his brother and said: "Here is what I want. I want to be at your five-year sobriety party. Specifically. I want to be there, somewhere outdoors, I think in summer, and I want you to be standing up saying something to a group of people. Your daughter will be nine. She will be old enough to understand what the five years meant. And I want to be sitting next to Mom, who will still be alive, and I want to watch her watch you say it. That is what I want."

He said nothing else about drinking. They talked about other things. The conversation ended.

Two weeks later, Caleb's brother checked into a residential treatment program. It has now been three years. Caleb's brother has told him twice, in private conversations, that it was the image of Mom watching him that he thought about during the worst week of withdrawal. He had pictured it in the detail Caleb described, and he could not stop picturing it.

How to deploy. When you want someone to make a difficult decision, describe the specific moment they will experience on the other side of it. Not the general outcome. A particular moment, with sensory detail. Where they will be. Who will be there. What time of day. What they will be doing. The richer the detail, the more the brain files it as something real.

The failure mode is abstraction. *Imagine how much better things will be* is a Preview that does nothing. It has no texture, no specifics, no anchor. A real Preview has edges. It could be a photograph.

Three tactics. Three ways of getting someone to reach a conclusion without being handed one. Notice what they share. In all three, the listener is doing the work of conviction. You are just providing the raw material.

*Field note: the best persuaders I know do not sound persuasive. They sound like people who have stories.*
