# Chapter 9

## The Path to Their Own Conclusion

This chapter closes Part Two. It covers four tactics that guide the listener to a specific conclusion through the path of their own thinking. Each tactic works by asking something, presenting something, or withholding something in a way that leaves the listener in a position to conclude one thing naturally.

The through-line of Part Two has been this: the best conversations end with the other person believing the outcome was their idea. These four tactics are how you engineer that outcome without the listener ever feeling guided.

### Tactic #21: The Discovery Path

Lawyers have a rule: never ask a witness a question you do not already know the answer to. In adversarial legal settings that rule protects against surprise. In persuasion settings, the same rule works differently. You ask questions you already know the answer to, not to avoid surprise, but to walk the listener down a path of their own logic to a conclusion that was always going to be at the end.

The Discovery Path is a calibrated sequence of questions, each of which can only be answered one way, leading to a conclusion the listener feels they arrived at. Done well, the listener is unaware that the sequence was constructed. They experience it as genuine inquiry. The conclusion at the end feels self-generated.

A founder I coached named Aisha was trying to raise $2M for her second round. She had a term sheet from a lead investor but needed a follow-on commit from an existing backer for the round to close. The existing backer had been evasive for two weeks. Aisha had a pitch meeting scheduled and was going to make her case directly.

I suggested she not pitch. Instead, ask four questions.

In the meeting, Aisha said: "Before I update you on the round, I want to ask you something. Do you think the category we are building in is going to be bigger or smaller than it was when you wrote your first check?"

The backer said bigger.

"Do you think we are further along now than you expected us to be at this point?"

The backer said yes.

"Do you think the new lead's involvement helps or hurts the company's trajectory?"

The backer said helps.

"Given all that, and given that the terms are substantially the same as the last round, is there any reason from your side not to participate at the pro rata?"

The backer paused. Then said, "No, there really is not."

He committed on that call. The round closed the following week.

The four questions were not manipulative. Each one was real. But the sequence mattered. Aisha had worked out the answers in advance, and she knew that once all four were stated out loud, there was no coherent position left from which to decline. The conclusion was the only thing that could follow.

How to deploy. When you want to lead someone to a specific conclusion, map backward from the conclusion. What three or four things would they have to agree with, in sequence, to reach it naturally? Phrase each as a question they can only answer one way. Ask them in order. Do not pitch between them.

The failure mode is pushing the sequence too fast. If the questions come too close together, or if the listener senses a trajectory, the whole thing collapses into a visible tactic. The questions should feel genuinely curious, not prosecutorial. Give them time between each one.

### Tactic #22: The Misdirected Question

Detective Columbo is the character this tactic is named after in my notes. His entire interrogation style was asking apparently peripheral questions that had nothing obvious to do with the crime, and listening for what the answer revealed. The criminal, relaxed by the apparent irrelevance of the question, would answer honestly, and in the honest answer would reveal exactly the fact they had been guarding.

The Misdirected Question works the same way in everyday conversation. You ask a question about something slightly off-center. The listener answers neutrally, not realizing that the answer contains the information you needed. The detector never fires, because the question was not about the thing the listener was defending.

A reader wrote to me about a situation with her boyfriend of two years. She had a strong suspicion he had been unfaithful while on a work trip two months earlier. Direct questions had produced defensive denial. She did not want to be wrong and accuse him of something he had not done. She also could not keep pretending she was not suspicious.

I told her to ask one misdirected question. Not about the trip. About something adjacent.

A few days later she asked him, at dinner, as if she had just thought of it: "Did Marcus end up going on that Chicago trip with you? I was talking to his wife yesterday and she said he'd been home all week but I could have sworn you said he was there too."

Her boyfriend went pale. Then said yes, Marcus had been there. Then a few seconds later said he needed to tell her something.

Marcus had not been in Chicago. Marcus had never gone on the trip. My reader had not talked to Marcus's wife. The entire setup was fabricated. The boyfriend's answer revealed that he was maintaining an alibi that depended on Marcus being there, which he had not realized she could verify. The affirmative answer to the misdirected question was the confession.

They have since separated. The misdirected question did not cause the separation. It surfaced something that had already happened. But it surfaced it without my reader having to accuse him, and without her being wrong about the underlying situation.

How to deploy. When you need to verify something someone is hiding, ask a peripheral question whose honest answer will reveal whether your suspicion is correct. The question must seem incidental. The answer must be verifiable independently, so you can know whether they lied.

The failure mode is asking a peripheral question that is too obviously a test. The misdirection has to be genuine. If the listener senses that the question is a probe, they will answer cautiously, and the signal will be lost. The best misdirected questions feel like passing curiosity.

### Tactic #23: The Two Doors

Psychologists have long observed that choice architecture matters more than the choices themselves. When people are presented with two options, they engage with the two. They rarely invent a third. Even when the third option is obvious, the fact that you gave them two creates a mental framework in which those are the only options on the table.

The Two Doors is the move of presenting exactly two options to someone, both of which lead to an outcome you are fine with, and letting them choose between them. The listener experiences themselves as making a choice freely. The third option, which is the outcome you do not want, is never introduced into the conversation.

A reader named Mark had been trying, for months, to get his seventeen-year-old son to apply to college early-decision. His son had been stalling on the application. Every direct conversation had turned into a fight about whether his son should even go to college at all. Mark had been arguing the case for college in general, which had put the specific decision about early-decision so far outside the conversation that it never got touched.

I told Mark to stop arguing about college. Set that question aside entirely. Next conversation, present two specific options.

Mark said to his son: "There are two things we could do with the next two weeks. We could sit down together on a Saturday and work through the Common App, or we could break it into pieces and you could handle the essay on your own while I handle the less-interesting logistical parts like transcripts and recommendations. Which one sounds less terrible to you?"

His son said the second one.

Two days later his son had started on the essay. Three weeks after that, he submitted his application. To the school his mother had suggested. Mark never argued about college again.

What had happened: by presenting two options for *how* to do the application, Mark had made *whether* to do it invisible. His son, presented with a choice about process, did not notice that the larger choice had been settled. He chose the option that appealed to him and moved forward.

How to deploy. When you need someone to make a decision you have an outcome preference on, present exactly two options, both of which lead to your preferred outcome. Frame them as genuine alternatives. Let the listener choose.

The failure mode is presenting two options that are not genuinely different. If the options are obviously versions of the same thing, the listener feels the trap and either picks nothing or invents the third option. The two must feel real to them, and they must actually be.

### Tactic #24: The Cinderella Frame

The scarcity principle in mainstream persuasion says to create urgency by signaling limited availability. Any modern listener knows this tactic. *Only two left in stock* fires the detector instantly.

The Cinderella Frame is the quieter version. You present something the listener should be interested in, and then, almost immediately, pull it back slightly. Not dramatically. Not with obvious scarcity language. Just enough that the listener moves from receiving toward pursuing. The name is an allusion: the thing they wanted appears for a moment and then withdraws, and they spend the rest of the evening looking for it.

A coaching client of mine named Nate (different Nate, no relation to me) had been in a dying text exchange with a woman he met through friends. They had exchanged numbers at a party, traded five or six messages over a week, and then the thread had gone flat. He was about to send a *hey, would love to get dinner sometime* message, which would have been his fourth unreciprocated overture in the thread.

I told him to send something different. A Cinderella Frame in a text.

He sent: "There is a bar near me that makes the second-best Negroni I have ever had. I was going to bring you there next week, but the bartender is in Barcelona until the 22nd and the substitute apparently slaughters the recipe. Rain check for later in the month."

She replied in seven minutes. Asked where the bar was. Asked about the first-best Negroni. Asked if he was free before the 22nd for something else. The thread was back. They went out the following Saturday.

What happened: Nate had offered something specific and appealing (a date at a specific place) and, in the same breath, pulled it back. The pullback was not a rejection; it was a delay with a reason. Her response was not the reactive resistance that a direct ask would have triggered. It was the pursuit behavior that only appears when something has almost been offered and then withdrawn.

How to deploy. When you want to offer something to someone in a way that invites pursuit, mention it and immediately introduce a reason the offer is not available right now. The pullback should be small and specific, not dramatic. You are not playing hard to get. You are letting them come forward.

The failure mode is overly theatrical pullback. Anything that looks like a deliberate scarcity move fires the detector. The Cinderella Frame is casual. Almost accidental. The listener should come away thinking you are simply someone whose time is not unlimited, which is true of everyone.

These four tactics close Part Two. They share the property that the listener does the cognitive work that produces the outcome you wanted. You provide the setup. They draw the conclusion, answer the question, make the choice, or begin the pursuit. In each, your role is architectural, not rhetorical.

*Field note: the best conversations end with the other person thanking you for helping them think clearly, without either of you being able to point to the sentence where the thinking happened.*
