# Chapter 16

## The Daily Practice

The tactics are over. The question is how you actually use them.

This chapter is short because the answer is simple. The complication is not in understanding. It is in restraint.

### Do not deploy more than two tactics in one conversation

The most common failure I see with my clients is over-deployment. They read the book, get excited, and walk into their next meeting prepared to run six tactics. They run all six. They close nothing.

The reason is what the whole book has been about. Visible tactics fire the detector. When you stack tactics, even the invisible ones start to feel like a system being run. Two tactics in a single conversation is plenty. One is usually better.

Choose based on where the conversation actually is. If the detector is firing in the first thirty seconds, use a Part One tactic and leave the others alone. If you need to plant a conclusion, use one Part Two tactic and let it work. Do not run the table.

### The seven-minute morning practice

Every morning, before your first real conversation of the day, spend seven minutes on three questions.

The first: what is the one conversation I am going to have today that matters? Not the calendar. The one where the outcome is uncertain and the stakes are real. Your brain can identify this in under a minute if you ask.

The second: what tactic best fits that conversation? Not two tactics. One. The one that addresses the specific problem. If the detector will fire, a Part One tactic. If you need them to reach a conclusion, a Part Two. If the frame is wrong, a Part Three. If the hard part is the ending, a Part Four.

The third: what will I do if the tactic does not land? The worst thing that happens when a tactic does not land is that you revert to visible persuasion and make the situation worse. Having a default posture of *say less, ask more, wait* prevents that drift.

Seven minutes. One conversation. One tactic. One fallback. That is the practice.

Do this for ninety consecutive days and the tactics start to become reflexive. You will not be thinking about them consciously. They will be available to you the way vocabulary is available to a fluent speaker.

### What to do when you get caught

Sometimes the other person will spot a tactic. Dana spotted me. It will happen to you.

When it does, the instinct is to deny, deflect, or double down. All three make it worse. The move is to confirm the spot, then stop.

"You are right. I was trying to land a pre-frame. Let me just talk straight."

That sentence does more for the relationship than any continued tactic could. It demonstrates that you are not committed to winning the conversation over the relationship. It often produces more trust than the tactic would have if it had worked. You get caught sometimes. When you do, confirm, and then do the simple thing.

### When to break every rule in this book

Most of the tactics in this book are contextual. They work in most high-stakes conversations and fail in specific ones. There are three situations where the book's rules do not apply.

The first is grief. When someone you care about is inside a real loss, the tactical frame does not fit. You sit with them. You listen. You do not deploy anything. The tactics work on people who have the bandwidth to be influenced. People in grief do not. They need witness, which is not a tactic.

The second is with children. My son is eight. I do not run tactics on him. Children are owed directness that adults have already earned the right to earn. Children develop their own detectors through experience, and deploying invisible influence on a child who cannot see it is not skill. It is theft.

The third is when your own integrity is at stake. There are conversations where the tactically-correct move is dishonest, and the honest move is tactically suboptimal. Choose integrity. The book is for winning conversations while preserving relationships. Not for preserving conversations while selling out relationships. If you find yourself running a tactic that requires you to be something you are not, stop.

### The long game

Most influence books treat the individual conversation as the unit of analysis. This book has too, for practical reasons. But the real unit is the reputation.

Every conversation you have contributes to a story that other people tell about what you are like to deal with. The story travels faster than you do. It shows up in rooms you have never entered. Someone who has talked to five people you know already has a read on you before they meet you.

The tactics in this book, used well, produce a specific reputation. People say you are easy to talk to. They say they do not quite know why, but they want you in the room. They say you are the person who listens. They say things work out when they work with you. They do not say you are persuasive. They do not say you are smart. They say something quieter and more durable.

That reputation is worth more than any individual deal. It compounds. Over ten years, it becomes the reason doors are open before you knock.

### What you do now

Go have a conversation.

Specifically: the first conversation you have today where the outcome matters, try one tactic. Just one. Read the tactic in this book ten minutes before the conversation. Pick a small, low-stakes moment to deploy it. Watch what happens.

Come back to the book tomorrow. Pick a different one.

In six weeks, come back to this chapter. Read it again. It will read differently then.

In six months, the tactics will be part of how you talk, and this book will be something you reference only when a specific problem comes up that you have not yet mapped. That is the right outcome. The book is not supposed to stay central. It is supposed to become the invisible layer underneath how you handle the conversations that matter.
