# Introduction

## How To Read This Book

This book is short on purpose. You can read it in an afternoon. You will use it for ten years.

The structure is simple. One origin chapter, one thesis chapter, four parts of tactical material, one closing chapter. Forty-seven numbered tactics, distributed across thirteen tactical chapters. An appendix at the end organized by situation.

Three things to know before you start.

### Do not memorize

If you try to memorize the tactics, you will deploy them, and deployment is the failure mode the whole book is designed to avoid. The tactics work when they have been absorbed to the point of being your natural behavior. That happens through exposure and practice, not through highlighting.

Read a tactic. Try it in a low-stakes conversation the same day. Notice what changes. Move to the next one. If a tactic does not click on first reading, skip it and come back later. Some of them only make sense after you have tried their neighbors.

### Read the story first, then jump

The first two chapters are not tactical. They are the setup. What happened to me. What the science says. Why every other persuasion book you have read has been training you to do the wrong thing.

If you picked this up because of a specific fight, a specific negotiation, or a specific person you are dealing with right now, read the first two chapters anyway. Then go directly to the appendix at the end of the book. The appendix is organized by situation. Find your situation. Read the tactics listed for it. Come back to the rest of the book later.

If you are not in a specific situation and you are reading to improve generally, go through the tactical chapters in order. Each chapter builds on the one before it. The parts are sequenced to match the arc of how a conversation actually unfolds, from the opening seconds to the moment after agreement.

### Expect the first reading to feel wrong

Almost every tactic in this book will feel counterintuitive the first time you read it. That is the design. If the tactics matched what your instincts already told you to do, they would not work, because everyone else would already be doing them.

Do not trust the tactics that sound obviously right. Pay attention to the ones that make you pause.

What you are reading is a re-reading of sixty years of psychology research, from the angle nobody looks from. Brehm wrote the foundational paper in 1966. Cialdini published *Influence* in 1984. Voss published *Never Split the Difference* in 2016. The research is not new. The reframe is.

Turn the page.

Your conversations are about to change.
