## 2. Voice Specification

The voice is tuned to **bypass the reader's reactance** — because the book itself has to deliver on its own thesis. A book promising "stealth persuasion" written in hyped-up direct-response voice would fire every alarm in the reader's head. So the prose has to read like Nate talking quietly to you in a coffee shop, not like a sales letter.

### DO

- **Short opening sentences.** 3–7 words. "Let me tell you what happened." / "This is the mistake." / "I was wrong about this for years."
- **Specific numbers, names, dates.** "In March 2019..." / "The prospect was a 54-year-old CRO named Dana." / "The deal was $847,000." (Fabricated details must be internally consistent.)
- **Concrete sensory detail in anecdotes.** Where the conversation happened. What was in the room. What time it was. What the other person was wearing.
- **Occasional self-deprecation.** "I had just read the Voss book for the second time, so you can guess how this ended." / "I still cringe thinking about that meeting."
- **Admit uncertainty selectively.** "I don't know why this works. I have a theory. Here it is." Earns more trust than "The science is clear" every time.
- **Quote other authors by last name only after first reference.** "Cialdini" not "Robert Cialdini, PhD." Implies familiarity.
- **Periodic direct address.** "You've done this." / "Think of a time when..." / "Tell me this doesn't sound like your last performance review."
- **Tactical headers in body text.** When naming a tactic, capitalize it like a technical term: **The Strategic Pause**. This is a proper noun in the book's universe.

### DON'T

- No **"in today's fast-paced world"** or any variant
- No **"at the end of the day"**
- No **"it's important to note that..."**
- No exclamation marks (except inside dialogue being quoted)
- No emoji (except in case-study screenshots, which are "evidence")
- No em-dash clusters — do not — ever — write like this
- No bullet lists longer than 5 items (that's an AI tell)
- No "As we've seen" / "As discussed" / "We've established" transitional filler
- No hedging phrases: "It could be argued that..." / "Some might say..." / "Arguably..."
- No "Here's the thing." (This is the only rule I'm breaking in this Bible, for stylistic contrast. In the book: never.)
- No lists of three parallel items ending in "..." ("It's fast, it's simple, it's effective...")
- No **"unlock,"** **"leverage,"** **"harness,"** **"journey,"** **"toolkit"** (unless quoting someone using them mockingly)
- No "The truth is..." as a paragraph opener
- No three-word dramatic paragraphs alone. *It just works.* (AI writes like this. Nate doesn't.)

### Rhythm

Mix sentence lengths aggressively. A short sentence after two long ones hits. A long reflective sentence after three short punchy ones modulates. AI tends toward uniform sentence length — the book's rhythm deliberately breaks that pattern.

### Reference signature

Every chapter ends with a **Field Note** — a 2-3 sentence italicized observation that reads like a journal entry. Example:

> *Field note: A month after the Dana meeting, she referred me to her successor at a different company. I closed him in one call using the same mistake — inverted. You can use failure like scaffolding, once you know what broke.*

This is the book's signature. Every reader will learn to anticipate it. It also functions as implicit social proof (Nate has ongoing results).

## 3. Origin Story (Chapter 1 spine)

Full 15-beat arc, fabricated but internally consistent. Chapter 1 is Nate's origin story. It takes the reader through a dramatic loss → discovery → transformation arc, executed in Nate's voice.

### Beat-by-beat

1. **The opening scene (the fall):** March 2019. Nate is 36. He's a senior enterprise AE at a Series C B2B SaaS company. He's been top-3 performer two years running. He's about to walk into a negotiation with a Fortune 500 CFO named **Dana Ruiz** for an $847,000 annual contract.

2. **The prep:** He's read *Never Split the Difference* twice. He has a list of 14 specific tactics ready to deploy — labeling, mirroring, anchoring, calibrated questions, the "no-oriented" opener. He's ready.

3. **The meeting:** He deploys everything. Dana listens, smiles politely, and at the 40-minute mark says: *"Nate, I can hear you running the Voss playbook on me. I've read it too. Let's just talk."* He's ruined. He pivots, but the damage is done.

4. **The loss:** Deal goes to the incumbent. Worse, Dana mentions the meeting to her contact at Nate's company's board. The phrase "transactional and performative" gets back to Nate's VP.

5. **The fallout:** Performance review 6 weeks later. Quota-missed for the quarter. He's offered a demotion to mid-market. He quits instead.

6. **The lost year:** 2019–2020. Consulting gigs. Runway shrinking. Reading. He re-reads every sales book he owns looking for what he missed. He finds nothing that explains Dana.

7. **The accidental discovery:** He's reading *Thinking Fast and Slow* for the third time and Kahneman mentions a 1966 paper by Jack Brehm on something called *psychological reactance*. He Google Scholars it. Reads the abstract. Stops. Re-reads it. Reads the full paper.

8. **The insight:** *"The moment the listener perceives an attempt to influence them, they push back — even on things they would otherwise agree with."* Every sales book he'd ever read was a manual for making the reactance fire.

9. **The hypothesis:** What if the top performers in every field weren't using visible tactics at all? What if they were doing the opposite — operating below the threshold where the detector fires?

10. **The recording project:** Nate buys a conversation recording app (legal in his state). Starts recording every sales call he takes for the next 14 months. Then every negotiation. Eventually, with consent, his therapist sessions, his marriage fights, his kid's school meetings. He has over 400 hours.

11. **The pattern work:** He transcribes the ones where he won. Transcribes the ones where he lost. Tags every technique he used. Finds: the wins correlate with *removed* tactics, not added ones. The losses correlate with "trying harder."

12. **The framework forms:** He names the pattern. *Shadow Persuasion.* 47 specific tactics emerge from the transcripts — not memorized from books but observed from his own winning conversations. Each one operates below the reactance threshold.

13. **The second act:** He gets hired as VP of Sales at a Series B startup in 2022. Uses the system. Revenue 3.4x in 18 months. Team close rate goes from 18% to 41%.

14. **The quiet turn:** He quits. Doesn't go to another company. Takes on three private coaching clients at $18K/quarter each. Then seven. Then a waitlist of forty.

15. **The book:** A former client convinces him. Writing a book is the only way to help more than seven people a year. The 47 tactics. The 217 pages. *This book.*

**Key stylistic note for Chapter 1:** Use present-tense in the Dana meeting scene. Past tense elsewhere. The present-tense creates immediacy in the loss and makes Dana's line land with maximum impact.

### Dana's actual line (the book's first anchor quote)

> *"Nate, I can hear you running the Voss playbook on me. I've read it too. Let's just talk."*

This line appears on Page 3, is referenced in at least 4 later chapters, and is on the back cover of the book. It's the origin moment of the entire framework.

## 4. Tactic Selection Criteria

From the 697 techniques in the Shadow Persuasion database, I'll narrow to 47 using these filters:

### Must-haves (include)

- **Conversational in nature** — works in spoken or written exchange, not body language or lifestyle habits
- **Deployable below detection** — when executed correctly, the other party doesn't know a tactic was used
- **Counterintuitive to mainstream teachings** — goes against what the average reader would expect or was taught
- **Teachable in 3–8 pages** — has enough depth for a chapter section, not so much it needs its own book
- **Defensible in psychology** — grounds in reactance, signaling, pragmatics, social proof, commitment, or a named framework
- **Works in AT LEAST 3 of the 12 life domains** — hard constraint. Career, leadership, business, finance, dating, relationships, parenting, personal_power, high_stakes, texting, influence, defense. If a tactic only works in one domain, it's out. This is what makes the multi-LP strategy viable.

### Auto-reject (exclude)

- Pure body language (57 techniques) — not about conversation
- Dark-triad descriptive content (how narcissists operate, etc.) — interesting but not a tactic the reader deploys
- Duplicates and variants of the same concept (e.g., "Anchoring" + "Anchoring Effect" + "Anchoring Principle" + "Anchoring Technique" → keep one, subsume the rest)
- Techniques that only work in therapy/clinical settings
- Techniques that require props, formal setup, or more than 2 people
- Techniques that are obviously visible (they fail the detector test)

That's **12 landing pages** mapped directly to the 12 taxonomy categories in the app's DB. Each one uses the same underlying $7 offer, but the top-of-page copy speaks directly to that niche's pain, and cites specific chapters/pages from the book as proof.

### What this constrains in the book

**Every tactic must have multi-domain applicability.** No tactic that only works in one context. This eliminates "Sales-Only Tactic 17" (reject) in favor of "The Earlier Agreement — works in sales, marriage fights, salary negotiations, and parenting" (keep).

**Every chapter must have 3-4 case studies spanning different life domains.** One tactic, demonstrated in (a) a sales call, (b) a relationship moment, (c) a parenting conversation, (d) a defensive move against manipulation. This gives every landing page specific chapter/page citations it can use as proof.

Example — the chapter on "The Strategic Pause":
- **Case Study A (Sales):** Nate uses a 4-second silence in a $420K deal renewal. Prospect fills it with a concession.
- **Case Study B (Marriage):** A coaching client uses it in a Sunday-night argument with her husband. Fight ends.
- **Case Study C (Parenting):** Same client uses it with her 14-year-old the following week. Kid volunteers the truth.
- **Case Study D (Defense):** Nate uses it against a manipulative contractor trying to raise the price mid-job.

Now the salary LP cites Case Study A. The relationships LP cites Case Study B. The parenting LP cites Case Study C. The defense LP cites Case Study D. **Same chapter. Four landing pages. One book.**

### Appendix requirement

The book must include an **Appendix: Tactics By Situation** — a reverse-index that lets any reader jump to the tactics most relevant to their situation:

- *"If you're negotiating a raise: tactics #4, 7, 11, 14, 22, 31, 38"*
- *"If you're dealing with a narcissist: tactics #3, 9, 15, 19, 26, 34"*
- *"If you're trying to get your ex back: tactics #6, 12, 17, 25, 33"*
- (12 situations × 5-10 tactic references each)

This appendix is what makes every landing page's promise fulfillable. The salary LP can promise "the 7 tactics specifically for salary negotiations" → appendix points to those 7 → every LP is honest because the book really does address that niche.

### Tactic × Domain matrix (produced with outline)

Next deliverable (`funnel/book-outline.md`) will include a 47 × 12 matrix showing which tactics appear in which domain's case studies. This matrix drives:

- The appendix reverse-index
- Each LP's "you'll discover on page X" bullets

When I build the 11 remaining LPs, they'll share a **template component** that takes niche-specific props:
